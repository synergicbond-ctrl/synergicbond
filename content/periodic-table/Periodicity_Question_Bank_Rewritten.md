## Solutions to Section 2.2

S1. **Answer: (a).** Across Period 2, first ionisation enthalpy generally increases. Boron is lower than beryllium because the electron removed from B is a higher-energy $2p$ electron, whereas Be has a stable filled $2s^2$ subshell. Therefore, $\text{B}<\text{Be}<\text{C}<\text{N}$.

S2. **Answer: (b).** Gallium is slightly smaller than aluminium because the intervening $3d$ electrons shield poorly, increasing $Z_{eff}$ on Ga. Down the rest of the group, additional shells increase size. Hence $\text{Ga}<\text{Al}<\text{In}<\text{Tl}$.

S3. **Answer: (b).** Zinc has $3d^{10}4s^2$, and its common ion $\text{Zn}^{2+}$ is $3d^{10}$. Neither has an incomplete d-subshell, so Zn is not a transition element under the strict definition.

S4. **Answer: (b).** Hf follows the lanthanoids. Poor shielding by the filled $4f$ subshell causes lanthanoid contraction, making the radii of Zr and Hf nearly identical.

S5. **Answer: (b).** Chlorine has the greatest electron-gain-enthalpy magnitude because its roomier $3p$ subshell experiences less electron-electron repulsion than F's compact $2p$ subshell. The order is $\text{Cl}>\text{F}>\text{Br}>\text{I}$.

S6. **Answer: (b).** Electronegativity increases with s-character because an s-orbital holds electron density closer to the nucleus. Since s-character follows $sp(50\%)>sp^2(33.3\%)>sp^3(25\%)$, the electronegativity order is $sp>sp^2>sp^3$.

S7. **Answer: (c).** $\text{Na}_2\text{O}$ is basic, $\text{SO}_3$ and $\text{Cl}_2\text{O}_7$ are acidic, while $\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3$ reacts with both acids and bases and is therefore amphoteric.

S8. **Answer: (d).** All four species contain 10 electrons. In an isoelectronic series, radius decreases as nuclear charge increases. Mg has the highest nuclear charge, so $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ is the smallest.

S9. **Answer: (b).** The inert-pair effect stabilises lower oxidation states down a p-block group. Thus Tl(I) is more stable than Tl(III).

S10. **Answer: (b).** Down Group 17, the H-X bond becomes longer and weaker, so proton release becomes easier. Therefore, aqueous acid strength follows $\text{HI}>\text{HBr}>\text{HCl}>\text{HF}$.

S11. **Answer: (b).** Adding an electron to $\text{O}^-$ requires overcoming repulsion from an already negatively charged ion. Consequently, the second electron gain enthalpy is always positive.

S12. **Answer: (a).** Be²⁺ is smaller and has greater polarising power than Mg²⁺. It distorts the electron cloud of Cl⁻ more strongly, making $\text{BeCl}_2$ more covalent.

S13. **Answer: (b).** Antimony lies along the conventional metal-non-metal boundary and is classified as a metalloid.

S39. $\text{CF}_3\text{COOH}$ is the stronger acid. S40. The effect is the inert-pair effect. Down groups 13 and 14, the outer $ns^2$ pair becomes progressively less willing to participate in bonding (poor shielding by intervening d/f electrons plus relativistic stabilisation), so the lower oxidation state (Tl(I), Pb(II)) becomes the thermodynamically preferred one. Consequently the higher states (Tl(III), Pb(IV)) are relatively unstable and tend to be reduced back to the inert-pair-favoured lower state — which is exactly what makes them oxidising agents.

S42. $\text{Na}_2\text{O}<\text{Al}_2\text{O}_3<\text{SO}_3$ in acidic character. Na (very low EN, strongly electropositive) forms a highly ionic, strongly basic oxide. Al sits near the metal/non-metal boundary; its oxide is amphoteric — sitting between basic and acidic. S, a non-metal in its highest (+6) oxidation state, forms a fully covalent, strongly acidic oxide (reacts with water to give $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$). This tracks the steady rise in central-atom electronegativity/oxidation state across the period.

S43. (a) HI vs HF acidity in water is bond-strength controlled: I is much larger than F, so the H–I bond is far longer/weaker than H–F, making H⁺ easier to release from HI — this happens despite F being far more electronegative than I. (b) HF vs $\text{CH}_4$ (C–H) is electronegativity controlled: within the same period/similar bond lengths, F's much higher electronegativity polarises the H–F bond far more strongly, favouring proton release, while C–H is barely polarised at all. The key point: the same-looking comparison ("which acid is stronger") is governed by an entirely different physical factor depending on whether you're comparing down a group or across a period.

S49. By Fajans' rules, $\text{Li}^+$ (smallest, most highly-charge-dense alkali cation) polarises $\text{Cl}^-$ the most strongly, giving LiCl the most covalent character among the alkali chlorides; $\text{Cs}^+$ is large and weakly polarising, so CsCl stays closest to purely ionic bonding. Greater covalent character generally lowers melting point (more molecular-like, less extended lattice character), so despite naive charge/size reasoning suggesting the small $\text{Li}^+$ should give the strongest lattice, the covalency effect dominates and CsCl has the higher melting point of the two (consistent with real values: LiCl ≈ 605°C, CsCl ≈ 645°C).

S50. $\text{Li}^+$'s small size gives it a very high charge density, so it attracts and binds a thick, tightly-held shell of water molecules — its effective (hydrated) radius moving through solution ends up larger than $\text{Cs}^+$'s. $\text{Cs}^+$, despite being physically bigger as a bare ion, is weakly hydrated and drags a much thinner water shell, giving it a smaller effective size and therefore higher mobility. Bare-ion size and effective (hydrodynamic) size are simply two different quantities.
